Reflecting on My Education

Jared Garalde on

April 23, 2022

I am a first-generation Filipino-American. I have had the privilege to pursue higher education because of my parents who immigrated here from the Philippines in the 1970’s. Since I was born, I was always encouraged to be true to myself and pursue the things that made me happy. I recognize that this is a privilege that not many people get to experience. However, that is why I am so passionate about giving back to underrepresented communities. I believe that if I, a 5’6” brown man, can create a meaningful impact in my field while battling the struggles of identity, racism, and mental health, it will inspire others to do the same. I was not always proud of being Filipino, or Asian for that matter. Growing up in a predominantly white community, the only connection to my heritage was through my family. I would be teased for my height, the color of my skin from my friends who were white, and I would be teased for being too “white-washed” from my friends who were minorities. I had no sense of identity, and the concept of being proud of where I was from was something that did not often occupy my mind.

This lack of identity contributed to my experience with mental health, something I have struggled with since I was in high school. I was diagnosed with clinical depression at 18, and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder at 21. This was something that I had trouble acknowledging for a long time. I would only tell my closest friends and family. I had attempted suicide twice. Those experiences, however, motivated me to regain control of my life. This started my journey of self-acceptance, and I began to take small steps and implement strategies that would help me find joy and purpose. One of the strategies I would use was to list things I was good at. I’ll admit, this was hard to do at first, but two of the qualities I came up with stuck with me. The first was that I was knowledgeable in technology and programming, and second, I had an eye for art and design. Naturally, I began to turn to these as an avenue to bolster my positivity and attitude toward my life. I immersed myself in design, filling sketchbooks and researching new methods. I explored different avenues, such as web design, illustration, painting, sculpture, and music. I loved discovering new ways to express myself. I discovered communities that shared the same interests as me. When I came to college, I was once again faced with a similar situation as when I was growing up. Washington State University is a predominantly white institution, and although I made lifelong connections my first semester, I felt like there was something I was missing.

I loved discovering new ways to express myself.

In my second semester, I found that something in the Filipino-American Student Association (FASA). I was introduced to people I related to on a level that I had never experienced before. I was learning more about my culture than I ever had before. I was attending events that celebrated my culture and sharing those experiences with others. I was, and am, proud to be Filipino-American. My involvement in FASA introduced me to the greater multicultural community through the Office of Multicultural Student Services on the fourth floor of our union building. I would spend probably 70% of my college experience there. I met a diverse range of students with different stories and experiences, some similar, and some vastly different from mine. I found mental health support groups that I could speak openly about my experiences with depression and suicide. I would attend events from held by organizations ranging from the Black Student Union to the Gender Identity and Sexual Orientation Resource Center. Just as I was proud to share my own culture, other people were as well. The more and more I became involved, the more I became motivated to pursue leadership in these organizations. I began to realize that despite my own love for these communities on campus, many people did not know about them.

The issues that faced these organizations were complex and dynamic, and I constantly had to learn more and meet new people to gain a better understanding of them. I wasn’t a great leader and could barely speak in front of an audience. However, the support I got from my peers was unprecedented. They pushed me to pursue positions such as President of my Asian-based fraternal organization, Vice President of the Filipino-American Student Association in Pullman, and Public Relations for the Asian-Pacific American Student Coalition. I began reaching out past campus on a state and national level. I began involving myself with the Be the Match program as a student representative, as well as the Filipino American Historical Society. The more people I would meet, the more my conviction grew to bring awareness to these groups so they could help someone struggling with their identity like I was. I began to apply my experiences in leadership and community involvement in my education. I would gear my projects toward solving issues of underrepresented communities. This ultimately led me to combine my interests in design, technology, and community involvement, in a career goal in user experience. It was a perfect and natural fit for me, and I would independently educate myself in any way I could.
